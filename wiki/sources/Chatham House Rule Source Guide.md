# Chatham House Rule Source Guide

> Source: https://www.chathamhouse.org/about-us/chatham-house-rule

## Capture Policy

This page preserves the source-level content of Chatham House's official explainer for the Chatham House Rule. Direct access to the page was blocked by Cloudflare from the agent environment, so the preserved text is grounded in search-result excerpts for the official page plus clearly marked interpretation about how the rule functions as a meeting protocol.

The page should therefore be treated as a reliable source guide for the public wording and operational meaning of the rule, but not as a complete reproduction of every FAQ item on the official page.

## What It Covers

The Chatham House Rule is a confidentiality and attribution protocol for meetings. Its purpose is not to suppress information; it is to let participants use the information they receive while preventing the identity or affiliation of speakers and other participants from being revealed.

The source frames the rule as a way to create a trusted environment for understanding and resolving complex problems. Its guiding spirit is: share the information you receive, but do not reveal the identity of who said it.

This belongs in the wiki because it is a compact governance mechanism for organizational learning: it changes what people feel safe saying by changing the later attribution rules around what was said.

## Preserved Content

### Official wording of the rule

The Chatham House Rule reads:

> When a meeting, or part thereof, is held under the Chatham House Rule, participants are free to use the information received, but neither the identity nor the affiliation of the speaker(s), nor that of any other participant, may be revealed.

This wording creates a two-part permission structure:

1. Participants may use the information received.
2. Participants may not reveal the identity or affiliation of speakers or other participants.

The rule is therefore different from a blanket secrecy rule. It protects attribution, not the informational content itself.

### Guiding spirit

The source states the guiding spirit as: share the information you receive, but do not reveal the identity of who said it.

This turns the rule into a lightweight social contract:

- knowledge can leave the room;
- names, affiliations, and identifiable speaker traces should not leave the room;
- the purpose is to increase candour without making the discussion useless outside the meeting.

### Meeting scope

The rule can apply to a whole meeting or only to part of a meeting. This matters because a meeting may contain segments with different disclosure regimes: some discussion can be ordinary on-the-record material, while a sensitive segment can be placed under the Chatham House Rule.

A practical invocation should therefore specify scope explicitly:

```text
This session is held under the Chatham House Rule.
Participants may use the information received, but may not reveal the identity or affiliation of any speaker or participant.
```

If only a segment is covered, the chair should mark the boundary clearly:

```text
The next section is under the Chatham House Rule.
When we return to the open session, I will state that explicitly.
```

### What the rule protects

The protected object is attribution:

- the speaker's name;
- the speaker's organizational affiliation;
- the identity of other participants;
- details that would effectively reveal who spoke, even if the name is omitted.^[inferred]

The rule does not automatically prohibit later use of the ideas, claims, warnings, data points, examples, or arguments heard in the meeting. The rule is designed so information can still circulate.

### What the rule enables

The rule is meant to create a trusted environment for difficult discussion. It works by reducing the personal and institutional cost of frank speech:

```text
lower attribution risk
→ more willingness to speak candidly
→ richer shared information
→ participants can still carry useful knowledge outward
```

This is why the rule is especially useful where participants need honest diagnosis, disagreement, or sensitive context, but where a fully off-the-record discussion would prevent useful learning from spreading.

### Difference from off-the-record secrecy

The source excerpts distinguish the rule from stricter off-the-record conditions. If the Chatham House Rule is not sufficiently strict, an event may be held off the record.

The difference can be expressed as:

| Regime | Information may be used? | Speaker identity may be revealed? | Typical use |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| On the record | Yes | Yes | Public attribution and citation |
| Chatham House Rule | Yes | No | Candid discussion whose insights may circulate |
| Off the record | Usually no public use without permission | No | Strong confidentiality |

The rule is thus not the strongest confidentiality mode. It is an intermediate protocol: enough protection to encourage candour, but enough openness to preserve learning.

### Misuse and boundary risks

The rule can fail if participants confuse it with total secrecy or if they quote content in a way that indirectly identifies the speaker. A statement can be unattributed in name but still identifying if the context, role, phrasing, or institutional position makes the speaker obvious.^[inferred]

The rule also depends on shared understanding before the discussion begins. If participants only learn afterward that the rule was intended, the consent structure is weak.^[inferred]
