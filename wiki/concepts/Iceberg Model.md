# Iceberg Model

The Iceberg Model is a systems-thinking lens that separates visible events from the deeper patterns, structures, and mental models that make those events recur. ^[inferred]

## Layers

- **Events** are the visible incidents people first notice: a crisis, a failure, a number, a conflict, or a symptom. ^[inferred]
- **Patterns** ask whether similar events have happened before and what trend connects them over time. ^[inferred]
- **Structures** ask what rules, incentives, information flows, feedback loops, boundaries, and resource arrangements keep producing the pattern. ^[inferred]
- **Mental models** ask what assumptions, goals, identities, and beliefs make the structure seem natural or necessary. ^[inferred]

## Use

The model changes the first question from “what happened?” to “what keeps making this happen?” It is close to [[wiki/topics/Thinking in Systems]] because both treat recurring outcomes as products of structure, not isolated willpower or accident. ^[inferred]

It also helps locate [[wiki/concepts/Leverage Points]]: event-level fixes usually react to symptoms; pattern, structure, and mental-model work moves closer to high-leverage change. ^[inferred]

## Practical Questions

- What event is visible right now?
- What pattern repeats across time?
- What structure makes the pattern likely?
- What mental model makes the structure acceptable?
- Which layer am I trying to change?
